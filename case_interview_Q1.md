# Case Study - Task 1: Market Participant Inquiry Response

## The Task

**You are a Market Surveillance analyst. You have received the following question from a market participant. What would you respond? Please be ready to motivate your answer.**

---

### Market Participant's Question:

> Dear Market Surveillance,
> 
> I have a question regarding bidding into the day-ahead and intraday market. During the past weeks, we have noticed that the intraday prices in bidding zone X have on average been 10% higher than prices in the day-ahead market.
> 
> As we are a power producer (active in both markets), we would like to reflect this price difference in our bids, pricing at 10% higher in the day-ahead. Could you please clarify what is allowed under REMIT regulation from the capacity withholding point of view and advise on best trading practices with respect to bidding in both day-ahead and intraday markets?
> 
> Best regards,
> Market Participant

---

## Response to Market Participant

Dear Market Participant,

Thank you for your inquiry regarding bidding practices across day-ahead and intraday markets. I appreciate your proactive approach to ensuring compliance with REMIT regulations.

### Short Answer

**No, you cannot systematically inflate your day-ahead bids by 10% based on observed intraday price premiums.** This would constitute capacity withholding and potential market manipulation under REMIT Article 5.

---

## Detailed Explanation

### 1. REMIT Regulatory Framework

**Article 5 - Prohibition of Market Manipulation**

REMIT explicitly prohibits any transaction or order to trade that:
- Gives false or misleading signals about supply, demand, or price
- Secures the price at an artificial or abnormal level
- Employs fictitious devices or any form of deception

**Capacity Withholding Definition:**

Capacity withholding occurs when a market participant intentionally:
- Offers available generation capacity at prices significantly above marginal costs
- Restricts supply to create artificial scarcity
- Aims to influence market prices to their advantage

### 2. Why Your Proposed Strategy Is Problematic

**Your stated intention reveals several red flags:**

| Issue | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Systematic inflation** | Inflating bids by 10% purely based on expected intraday premiums (not costs) is artificial pricing |
| **Intent to withhold** | Your goal is to price yourself OUT of day-ahead to capture higher intraday prices |
| **Supply manipulation** | Removes available capacity from day-ahead, potentially driving prices up |
| **Market distortion** | Prevents efficient price formation and resource allocation |

**What makes this capacity withholding:**
- You have available generation capacity
- Your bids are not based on actual costs or legitimate technical constraints
- You're intentionally restricting supply from one market to profit in another
- This is systematic behavior (not a one-time commercial decision)

### 3. Legitimate vs. Illegitimate Bidding Practices

#### ✅ **ALLOWED - Legitimate Bidding Strategies**

**Cost-based bidding:**
- Bid at or above your actual marginal cost of production
- Include legitimate operational costs (start-up, ramping, fuel, carbon)
- Account for technical constraints (minimum run times, ramp rates)

**Portfolio optimization:**
- Balance positions across markets based on risk management
- Respond to actual supply/demand fundamentals
- Adjust for reservoir management (hydro) or fuel constraints

**Market-based pricing:**
- Bid competitively based on market conditions
- Reflect opportunity costs when capacity is genuinely limited
- Price based on expected scarcity during your generation period

#### ❌ **NOT ALLOWED - Market Manipulation**

**Artificial price inflation:**
- Systematically bidding above costs to force higher clearing prices
- Coordinating withholding across multiple units to reduce supply
- Gaming the market to create artificial scarcity

**Economic withholding:**
- Inflating bids purely to capture price differences between markets
- Restricting supply without legitimate commercial justification
- Your explicitly stated strategy falls into this category

### 4. Understanding Day-Ahead vs. Intraday Price Differences

**Why do intraday prices differ from day-ahead?**

The 10% premium you've observed likely exists due to **legitimate market factors:**

| Factor | Effect on Intraday |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Forecast errors** | Wind/solar output deviations → supply/demand imbalances |
| **Unplanned outages** | Generator trips create unexpected shortages |
| **Demand variations** | Actual consumption differs from forecasts |
| **Balancing needs** | System operators need real-time adjustments |
| **Transmission constraints** | Congestion patterns emerge closer to delivery |

**Important:** These premiums reflect **real physical constraints and information updates**, not opportunities for strategic withholding.

### 5. Best Trading Practices

**What you SHOULD do:**

1. **Bid honestly in day-ahead:**
   - Offer your available capacity at prices reflecting your actual costs and commercial strategy
   - Consider your full generation portfolio and risk position
   - Use day-ahead as your primary market for base commitments

2. **Use intraday appropriately:**
   - Adjust positions based on NEW information (forecast updates, outages, etc.)
   - Balance your portfolio closer to real-time
   - Trade in intraday when your operational situation genuinely changes

3. **Document your bidding rationale:**
   - Maintain clear records of cost calculations
   - Document technical constraints affecting bids
   - Show legitimate commercial reasoning for pricing decisions

4. **Avoid systematic patterns:**
   - Don't automatically inflate bids based solely on historical price differences
   - Each bid should reflect conditions for that specific delivery period
   - Be prepared to justify significant deviations from costs

### 6. Surveillance Perspective

**What Market Surveillance monitors:**

Our systems routinely detect:
- Bids systematically above historical cost estimates
- Capacity offered in intraday but withheld from day-ahead
- Correlation between withholding behavior and price impacts
- Patterns indicating economic withholding

**If we observe your proposed behavior:**
- Automatic alerts would trigger for investigation
- We would analyze bid-to-cost ratios across your portfolio
- Pattern recognition would identify systematic withholding
- This could lead to formal investigation and NRA notification

### 7. Consequences of Non-Compliance

**Regulatory penalties:**
- Formal investigation by National Regulatory Authority (NRA)
- Fines up to €1 million or 10% of annual turnover (whichever is higher)
- Potential trading restrictions or market bans
- Mandatory compliance programs

**Commercial risks:**
- Reputational damage in the market
- Loss of trust with market operators and other participants
- Potential civil liability to affected market participants
- Strategy may backfire if premiums disappear (market self-corrects)

---

## Recommended Approach

### What You Can Do Instead:

1. **Analyze WHY intraday premiums exist**
   - Are there systematic forecast errors in your bidding zone?
   - Is there structural under-supply in specific hours?
   - Understanding drivers helps legitimate commercial strategy

2. **Optimize your portfolio legitimately**
   - Use day-ahead for base commitments
   - Reserve some flexible capacity for intraday opportunities
   - But this flexibility must be REAL (e.g., flexible hydro, peaking units)

3. **Improve your forecasting**
   - Better day-ahead bidding based on expected intraday conditions
   - Still bid honestly, but with better information
   - Competitive advantage through superior analysis, not manipulation

4. **Engage with market design**
   - If you believe systematic premiums indicate market design flaws
   - Provide feedback to Nord Pool and regulators
   - Participate in market development discussions

---

## Conclusion

**Direct Answer to Your Question:**

You **cannot** systematically inflate your day-ahead bids by 10% based on observed intraday premiums. This constitutes capacity withholding under REMIT and is market manipulation.

**Best practice:**
- Bid your available capacity honestly in day-ahead at prices reflecting your actual costs and legitimate commercial considerations
- Use intraday markets to adjust positions based on real information updates
- Maintain documentation justifying your bidding decisions

The intraday premium you observe reflects legitimate market dynamics. Attempting to exploit it through capacity withholding distorts markets, violates REMIT, and exposes you to significant regulatory and commercial risks.

If you have specific technical constraints or legitimate commercial reasons affecting your bidding strategy, I'm happy to discuss those in more detail to ensure you remain compliant while optimizing your trading approach.

**Market Surveillance Analyst**  
Nord Pool

## Motivation for This Answer

### Regulatory Basis

1. **REMIT Article 5** explicitly prohibits market manipulation, including capacity withholding
2. **ACER Guidance** on REMIT implementation clarifies that economic withholding (bidding above costs to restrict supply) is manipulation
3. **Case law** from NRA enforcement shows similar strategies have resulted in penalties

### Market Integrity Rationale

1. **Price formation:** Day-ahead prices must reflect actual supply/demand, not strategic withholding
2. **System reliability:** TSOs depend on accurate day-ahead commitments for grid planning
3. **Market efficiency:** All participants suffer when systematic manipulation distorts price signals

### Practical Considerations

1. **Detection probability:** High - surveillance systems specifically monitor for this pattern
2. **Strategy sustainability:** Low - markets adjust when participants systematically withhold
3. **Risk-reward:** Regulatory penalties far exceed potential gains from price arbitrage

The participant's explicit statement of intent to inflate bids makes this a clear-cut case of prohibited behavior that requires direct, unambiguous guidance.
